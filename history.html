D-Day: June 6, 1944

The Longest Day — When the course of history changed on Normandy's shores

Operation Overlord

On June 6, 1944, Allied forces launched the largest seaborne invasion in history along the Normandy coastline. Code-named Operation Overlord, it marked the beginning of the liberation of Western Europe from Nazi Germany.

Over 156,000 troops from the United States, United Kingdom, Canada, and other Allied nations stormed five beaches across a 50-mile stretch of heavily fortified coast. Supported by 5,000 ships, 13,000 aircraft, and 195,700 naval personnel, it was a pivotal moment in World War II.

By the end of August 1944, all of northern France was liberated, and the Allies were reorganizing for the final push into Germany.

Timeline of D-Day

The key moments of the longest day in history

00:15 — Night Drop

Airborne Assault Begins

Over 24,000 airborne troops parachute and glide into Normandy under cover of darkness to secure bridges and flanks ahead of the beach landings.

03:00 — Naval Bombardment

Allied Fleet Opens Fire

Over 5,000 vessels begin a massive naval bombardment of German coastal defences along the 50-mile invasion front.

06:30 — H-Hour

Beach Landings Begin

The first waves of infantry hit the beaches — Utah and Omaha (US), Gold and Sword (UK), and Juno (Canada). Omaha saw the heaviest resistance.

07:10 — Pointe du Hoc

Rangers Scale the Cliffs

225 US Army Rangers scale the 100-foot cliffs of Pointe du Hoc under enemy fire to destroy German gun emplacements threatening the beaches.

12:00 — Noon

Beachheads Secured

By midday, all five beachheads are established despite fierce resistance. Troops begin moving inland toward key objectives.

End of June 6

A Foothold in France

By midnight, 156,000 troops, 6,000 vehicles, and hundreds of tons of supplies are ashore. The liberation of Europe has begun.

August 25, 1944

Paris Liberated

After 80 days of fighting in Normandy, Allied forces liberate Paris. The tide of war has turned decisively against Nazi Germany.

The Five Landing Beaches

Each beach tells its own story of courage and sacrifice

Bloodiest Beach
🇺🇸 Omaha Beach

The costliest beach with over 2,000 American casualties. Home to the Normandy American Cemetery with 9,387 graves overlooking the sea.

Westernmost
🇺🇸 Utah Beach

The westernmost landing beach. Despite landing 2 km off target, the 4th Infantry Division suffered the fewest casualties — 197 out of 23,000.

British Sector
🇬🇧 Gold Beach

The British 50th Division landed here and pushed 6 miles inland by day's end, capturing the town of Arromanches for the vital Mulberry harbor.

Canadian Heroes
🇨🇦 Juno Beach

Canadian forces faced fierce resistance before breaking through. The Juno Beach Centre honors the 45,000+ Canadians who gave their lives in WWII.

Easternmost
🇬🇧 Sword Beach

The easternmost beach where the British 3rd Division landed. They pushed close to Caen but were met by the feared 21st Panzer Division.

Clifftop Assault
🎖️ Pointe du Hoc

Not a beach but a cliff. US Rangers used grappling hooks and ladders to scale 100-foot cliffs under fire — one of WWII's most daring actions.

Memorials & Museums

Sacred places of remembrance across Normandy

American Cemetery

9,387 graves on a bluff overlooking Omaha Beach. A deeply moving experience.

Caen Memorial

One of Europe's greatest WWII museums, dedicated to peace and reconciliation.

Pegasus Bridge

Captured by British glider troops minutes after midnight — the first objective of D-Day.

Overlord Museum

Houses a stunning collection of WWII vehicles, weapons, and personal artifacts.
